Whole Foods
Minimally processed, nutrient-dense eating
▼
🌿 Balanced Beginner Friendly Cycle-Friendly ★★★★★

Whole foods eating focuses on foods in their natural, minimally processed state — vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes, nuts, seeds, and quality animal products. There's no strict rulebook; the goal is to crowd out ultra-processed foods with real, recognizable ingredients. It's less a diet and more a philosophy of eating closer to nature. This approach is widely considered one of the most sustainable and health-promoting eating patterns across the scientific literature.

Pros
  • Easy to sustain long-term
  • Supports stable blood sugar
  • Rich in fiber for gut health
  • Reduces inflammation
  • No foods are off-limits
Cons
  • Requires more meal prep
  • Whole foods cost more
  • Less defined — can feel vague
  • Eating out is harder

Vegan
Zero animal products, entirely plant-based
▼
🌿 Plant-Based Ethical Choice Cycle-Friendly ★★★☆☆

Veganism eliminates all animal products — meat, dairy, eggs, and honey — relying entirely on plants for nutrition. When well-planned, it can be highly nutritious; when not, deficiencies are common. For women specifically, iron, B12, zinc, omega-3s, and calcium require careful attention. Hormonal health on a vegan diet depends heavily on whether you're getting adequate fat and protein, both of which are critical for estrogen and progesterone production.

Pros
  • High in antioxidants and fiber
  • Reduces saturated fat intake
  • Strong ethical/environmental case
  • May reduce inflammation
  • Lower risk of certain cancers
Cons
  • B12 must be supplemented
  • Iron absorption is lower (non-heme)
  • Low omega-3 (DHA/EPA)
  • Risk of hormonal disruption if under-eating fat/protein
  • Socially restrictive

Carnivore
Animal products only — meat, fish, eggs, dairy
▼
🥩 Animal-Based Zero Carb Cycle-Friendly ★★★☆☆

The carnivore diet consists exclusively of animal products — primarily meat, fish, eggs, and sometimes dairy. All plant foods are eliminated. Proponents report dramatic improvements in autoimmune conditions, digestive issues, and mental clarity. For women with food sensitivities, it can provide significant relief by eliminating common inflammatory plant compounds like lectins and oxalates. Hormonal impact depends heavily on fat intake — adequate saturated and monounsaturated fats are essential for steroid hormone production. Under-eating fat on carnivore can disrupt cycles.

Pros
  • Eliminates common food triggers
  • High satiety — less hunger
  • Dense in bioavailable nutrients
  • Supports muscle maintenance
  • Reduces autoimmune symptoms for many
Cons
  • No fiber — gut adjustment needed
  • Risk of cycle disruption if under-eating
  • Low in magnesium and Vitamin C
  • Socially challenging
  • Long-term research limited

Ketogenic
Very low carb, high fat, moderate protein
▼
🔥 Low Carb High Fat Cycle-Friendly ★★★☆☆

The ketogenic diet keeps carbohydrates extremely low (typically under 20–50g/day) to shift the body into ketosis — a metabolic state where fat becomes the primary fuel source instead of glucose. It's highly effective for fat loss and blood sugar control. For women, keto requires careful attention: the female body is more hormonally sensitive to carbohydrate restriction than men's, and some women experience cycle disruption, especially at very low carb thresholds. A targeted or cyclical keto approach (adding carbs around ovulation/luteal phase) often works better for women.

Pros
  • Powerful fat-burning state
  • Reduces insulin resistance
  • Suppresses appetite significantly
  • Mental clarity and stable energy
  • Helps with PCOS symptoms
Cons
  • Can disrupt cycles in women
  • "Keto flu" adaptation period
  • Restrictive and socially hard
  • Electrolyte loss (sodium, magnesium)
  • Not ideal for intense exercise

Mediterranean
Olive oil, fish, vegetables, whole grains
▼
⚖️ Balanced Research-Backed Cycle-Friendly ★★★★★

The Mediterranean diet is consistently ranked among the most evidence-backed eating patterns in the world. It emphasizes olive oil, fish, vegetables, legumes, whole grains, and moderate wine — with red meat eaten sparingly. For women's hormonal health, it scores extremely well: high in anti-inflammatory omega-3s, rich in phytonutrients, moderate in carbohydrates, and naturally supportive of estrogen metabolism. It's also one of the most sustainable diets long-term, as it's genuinely enjoyable and doesn't require elimination of whole food groups.

Pros
  • Strongest research backing of any diet
  • Anti-inflammatory by nature
  • Excellent for heart and brain health
  • Supports all cycle phases well
  • Flexible and enjoyable
Cons
  • Slower weight loss than low-carb diets
  • High-quality olive oil is expensive
  • Not ideal for those avoiding grains/legumes
  • Loose structure can lead to overeating

Paleo
Eat like our ancestors — no grains, legumes, dairy
▼
🍖 Animal-Based Grain-Free Cycle-Friendly ★★★★☆

Paleo mimics what our pre-agricultural ancestors ate: meat, fish, eggs, vegetables, fruits, nuts, and seeds — while eliminating grains, legumes, dairy, refined sugar, and processed foods. The reasoning is evolutionary: our bodies evolved before farming, so modern agricultural foods may contribute to inflammation and chronic disease. For women, paleo tends to be hormone-supportive due to its emphasis on quality protein and fat, though the elimination of legumes removes a useful fiber and phytoestrogen source that some women benefit from.

Pros
  • Eliminates processed foods naturally
  • High in quality protein and fat
  • Reduces blood sugar spikes
  • Good for autoimmune conditions
  • Nutrient-dense by design
Cons
  • Eliminates healthy legumes and grains
  • Can be expensive (quality meat)
  • Lower in calcium (no dairy)
  • Socially restrictive
  • Limited research vs. Mediterranean

Intermittent Fasting
Eating windows, not food restrictions
▼
⏱ Fasting Flexible Cycle-Friendly ★★☆☆☆

Intermittent fasting (IF) isn't about what you eat — it's about when. Common protocols include 16:8 (fast 16 hours, eat within an 8-hour window), 5:2 (normal eating 5 days, restricted calories 2 days), and OMAD (one meal a day). IF can be highly effective for weight management and metabolic health. However, women are significantly more hormonally sensitive to fasting than men. Extended fasting can elevate cortisol, suppress luteinizing hormone (LH), disrupt ovulation, and worsen PMS. A gentler 12:12 or 14:10 approach, with adjustments around the luteal phase, works much better for women.

Pros
  • No food group restrictions
  • Improves insulin sensitivity
  • Convenient — less meal prep
  • Supports autophagy (cell repair)
  • Can reduce overall calorie intake naturally
Cons
  • Can disrupt female hormones significantly
  • Worsens PMS in luteal phase
  • Risk of binge eating in eating window
  • Not recommended with history of disordered eating
  • Harder to adjust for social eating

Gluten-Free
Wheat, barley, and rye eliminated
▼
⚖️ Sensitivity-Based Celiac / NCGS Cycle-Friendly ★★★★☆

A gluten-free diet eliminates all sources of gluten — a protein found in wheat, barley, and rye. It is medically necessary for those with celiac disease, and many people with non-celiac gluten sensitivity (NCGS) or autoimmune conditions also report significant improvement. For women, undiagnosed gluten sensitivity can contribute to hormonal disruption, irregular cycles, and fatigue — particularly in the luteal phase. Going gluten-free can dramatically reduce systemic inflammation in sensitive individuals. When done well (not just substituting processed GF foods), it's a nutrient-dense and hormone-supportive approach.

Pros
  • Essential for celiac disease
  • Reduces inflammation in sensitive individuals
  • May improve cycle regularity
  • Reduces bloating and digestive issues
  • Encourages whole food choices
Cons
  • GF processed foods often low-nutrient
  • Risk of fiber and B-vitamin deficiency
  • More expensive grocery bill
  • No benefit if not gluten-sensitive
  • Cross-contamination risk eating out
